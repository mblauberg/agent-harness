# Argument Structure And Paragraphing

How to build a submission so each paragraph makes one point, nothing is re-argued, and a
judicial reader gets question-answer-reasons at a glance. Load with
`legal-concision-and-anti-ai.md` for any advocacy or submission work. This reinforces the
SKILL Concision Pass; it does not replace it. The boundary rules at the end govern: never
trade an honest legal negative for elegance.

## Write For The Decision-Maker

- The judgment-ready standard: draft submissions capable, if the tribunal agrees, of forming
  the substance of the reasons. That one test drives accuracy, restraint, issue-by-issue
  structure and citation discipline (Heydon: sufficient authority, not too much). Written
  submissions are the first half of the appeal; a bad start is hard to recover.
- Write for a busy generalist meeting the matter cold: assume less time, less record
  knowledge and less niche law than the drafter has. Explain and orient; never assume
  familiarity. The greatest sin is a reader who feels lost and must re-read.
- If the case turns on a statute, contract or order, get to its words fast. Long background
  before the operative text reads as avoidance - the suspicion is the wording is against you.
  Put the provision inside the point it governs, not in an early block the reader must flip
  back to.
- File or attach a chronology where the forum allows it; a dated, sourced chronology keeps
  narrative detail out of the argument body.

## Front-Load The Whole Document

- A fresh reader can state the relief sought, the grounds and why within about 90 seconds.
  The opening delivers the upshot before any detail.
- Deep issue: frame the central question as a syllogism of about 75 words or fewer, in
  separate sentences - legal premise, then concrete factual premise, then the question -
  concrete enough that a non-lawyer follows it. Put the premises before the question so the
  answer is implicit. One issue block, not a string of abstract "whether" clauses. State the
  issue concretely: embed the decisive fact ("whether the discretion miscarried where both
  eyewitnesses to the collision had died"), never "whether the tribunal erred", which tells
  the reader nothing.
- Make any summary-of-argument detachable: it reads alone as a complete persuasive document,
  issue by issue - your position in a sentence or two, the flaw in the other side's position,
  then the fuller why. Ideally it makes reading the rest optional.
- An arresting, concrete opening beats a recital: "There are four fundamental flaws in the
  appellant's contentions", then the flaws numbered, each footnoted to the record, is a
  worked gold standard. State the case as you would to an interested but distracted
  colleague; no jury speech.
- Opening order of operations, by scope. These front-loading devices compete for the same
  opening; pick by unit size, do not stack them. Whole document: upshot paragraph (relief +
  grounds + why), then the deep issue, then a detachable summary where length warrants one.
  Single ground or short section inside a larger document: open with the one-sentence
  ground-answer carrying the embedded decisive fact; use a deep-issue block only where the
  ground is long enough to need it; relief lives in the document's orders block, not in the
  section's first paragraph.

## Headings Carry The Argument

- Each argument-bearing heading is a one-sentence assertion of that section's conclusion, not
  a topic label. The heading list, read alone top to bottom, states the whole case.
- Test: extract every heading into a bare list. If a line is a neutral tag ("Background", "The
  completion date") rather than a claim ("The reasons disclose no finding on the agreed date"),
  it fails.
- Exception: genuinely structural sections - a procedural history, a shared-framework or
  umbrella block - may stay labels. Do not force a contention where the section only orients.

## One Point Per Paragraph

- Open every paragraph with its contention - a contestable position the reader could agree or
  disagree with - not a question or a recital. Conclusion first (CRAC/CREAC).
- One developed point per paragraph. If the honest one-line caption needs "X and (separately)
  Y", where Y could stand or fall alone, split into two. Tie-breaker against Synthesise
  Fact-Clusters below: instances that prove the same proposition merge into one paragraph;
  propositions that could each independently carry a ground or sub-point split.
- Close each ground or section on its conclusion in one or two sentences. Do not trail off on a
  citation or a fresh sub-point.
- In a rule paragraph, funnel: the broadest governing principle first, then the narrower
  qualifications and exceptions; order multiple authorities highest court to lowest.
- Put the most decisive sentence of a unit at its start or end (the emphatic positions); keep
  routine chronology in the interior.

## Synthesise Fact-Clusters Into One Proposition

- When several facts, messages, transactions or authorities each make the same small point, do
  not give each its own narrated sentence or paragraph with the conclusion repeated after every
  item. State the pattern and its single conclusion once as the paragraph's topic sentence, then
  hang the instances beneath it as a compressed anchor list (a semicolon series or lettered
  sub-paras), each keeping its own pinpoint.
- Test: does this paragraph exist only to add one more instance of a pattern the paragraph above
  already states? If yes, fold it in as a clause plus pinpoint, not a new paragraph.
- Draw the conclusion the cluster proves - "on his own contemporaneous words the sum had no
  settled existence" - rather than leaving the reader to add the instances up. A synthesised
  proposition with the facts marshalled under it reads stronger and shorter than a fact-by-fact
  recitation. Synthesise WITHIN one issue; never fuse distinct legal questions (jurisdiction,
  reasons, procedural fairness) into one "errors" block to save space - that destroys the
  separate-CRAC structure and makes each ground unwinnable.
- Embed the decisive fact in the issue itself: "whether the adjudicator erred by accepting a $X
  figure that embedded interest the claimant denied and later conceded", not "whether the
  adjudicator erred as to interest". One concrete issue line does the marshalling several
  abstract paragraphs attempt.

## State It Once; The One Allowed Echo

- Front-load once: state each shared premise, rule, figure and chronology once, in the
  framework or umbrella block, and have later sections cite it by name. (See the SKILL Concision
  Pass "one document does one job"; for the QCAT/QCATA gamma structure see the matter skill's
  `document-structures.md`.)
- Emphasis is not redundancy. Deliberate restatement is legitimate only at a structural
  primacy point - the opening frame and one close - and only as reinforcement that carries new
  evidence or stakes. Every other repeat is redundancy: cut it to a cross-reference.
- The reason is credibility, not only length: a repetitious filing is skimmed, and a reader who
  sees the same point three times discounts the writer. Cutting repetition is a persuasion move,
  not housekeeping.
- Protective formulas are rationed the same way. A recurring shield formula (a convergence
  theory, a context-only renunciation, a purpose-limit on sensitive material) appears in full at
  its defined home, its first grounds-body use and the per-ground application close; every other
  deployment folds to a bare node cite (`(C.1)`, `(C.7)`). By the closing parts a repeated formula
  reads as a tic, and repeated disclaiming draws the eye to the very material it disclaims. Two
  limits: no deployment site is ever left without at least the bare cite (the shield must survive
  everywhere it protects), and the one site that actually deploys the sensitive material keeps the
  full formula - reducing the shield at the point of use is a cut too far. Distinguish the
  renunciation echo (reduce) from a located evidentiary purpose-limit on a specific source (keep).
- Pack coherence (multi-document filings): when the filing is a pack - submissions plus an outline,
  schedules and a draft orders sheet - one point still has one home and every other document
  cross-refers to it. A cross-reference must name the target document and a stable locus (its
  heading, paragraph or schedule number, not a page number that re-flows on rebuild), and it must
  resolve to something that exists. The same figure, defined term, date or label must read
  identically in every document in the pack; reconcile them before filing, because a reader who
  finds the award total stated two ways in the one pack discounts both.

## Select And Aim

- Grounds-selection: run the strongest grounds, not every available one. A stack of marginal
  grounds signals under-analysis and dilutes the strong points. Appellate judges say this
  plainly: seven to ten grounds raises a presumption none has merit (Aldisert, adopted by
  McHugh J); the scattergun approach "obscures the true issues" and "camouflages the
  pleader's best points" (Ipp JA).
- Ground-tied diagnostic: every submission ties to a specific ground or issue. If a
  submission cannot be articulated cleanly and supported by record references and legal
  principle, the defect is usually in the ground itself - amend it or abandon it; do not
  paper over it with prose.
- One argument is one argument. Do not dress a single contention as a "primary limb" and an
  "alternative limb": false alternatives read as under-confidence and invite the tribunal to
  treat the point as weaker than it is. Reserve express alternatives for genuinely distinct
  legal routes to the relief.
- Do not double-number. If sub-sections are already numbered (E.2, E.3 ...), do not also label
  them "Limb 1", "Limb 2": two enumeration schemes for one structure read as machine scaffolding.
  Let the numbered headings carry the count and refer to the points in plain ordinals ("first ...
  secondly ... thirdly"). Reserve "limb" for a genuine term of art (the limbs of a two-part test,
  the division-of-assets limb of a binary); for enumerated strands under an already-numbered
  structure prefer a plain word ("aspect", "point"). Client-facing jargon such as "limb" or
  "strand" where a plainer word does the same job is an avoidable readability cost.
- Favourable-direction filter: every retained passage must move the tribunal toward the relief
  sought. If you cannot say with conviction why a passage helps, cut it.
- Primacy: lead with the strongest ground, and within a multi-limb ground lead with the
  strongest limb. Order threshold-first, then strongest-to-weakest - a potentially dispositive
  jurisdiction/nullity ground leads regardless of when it arose in the hearing.
- Cap live grounds at three, at most four; fold a weaker or overlapping complaint in as a
  sub-point of a stronger ground rather than as a co-equal heading. Over-issuing signals
  under-confidence.
- Cut versus relocate - keep the two distinct. Cut a genuinely weak point outright (a hedged
  "in the alternative" makeweight dilutes the strong grounds and signals they needed padding).
  But do NOT cut strong, cited figure or quantum substance to hit a page target: relocate it to a
  schedule or annexure with a pinpoint cross-reference. Cutting substance guts the evidentiary
  spine; relocating keeps the force and saves the page. Sentence-level trimming alone floors at a
  few pages - a large page cut comes from homing repeated analysis and relocating cited working,
  not from shortening sentences.
- Schedule-role discipline: a schedule - of authorities, of a calculation, of a reasons-deficiency
  - is a reference and record device, not a place to argue. The argument lives in the submissions;
  the schedule is the marshalled backup a submission points to at its point of use, cited by number
  where it does its work. Do not smuggle a contention into a schedule that the submissions do not
  make. Where several submissions in a wave draw on the same material, consolidate it into one
  standalone schedule they each cite rather than repeating it in each - the one-home rule applied to
  the pack's reference layer.
- Tables are a readability device, not a page-saver. Converting reasoned prose to a multi-column
  table almost never shortens the document and usually lengthens it: the cell text that preserves
  the argument (inference plus pinpoint) is paragraph-length, and paragraph-length text in a narrow
  column wraps heavily and carries cell padding, so it consumes more vertical space than the same
  words flowing full-width. Reserve tables for genuinely tabular material - short-cell data, a
  figure-to-source map, a compact matrix a time-pressed reader parses at a glance. If you table to
  aid comprehension, do it knowing it costs a page or two, not to save them. Real page savings come
  from prose synthesis (fold repeated instances into one proposition plus an anchor list) and
  relocation, not from re-laying prose into a grid. Measure page count on the actual render before
  claiming a table saved space.
- Density ceiling: a page limit is a ceiling, not a target, and saturation is its own defect. When
  a draft nears the forum's limit, the trigger is structural, not sentence-level: re-run
  grounds-selection (cut a weak ground outright), re-home repeated analysis to its one owner, and
  relocate cited working to a schedule - sentence trimming alone floors at a few pages. Length
  erodes persuasion before it breaches a rule: the longer the document, the less of it is read,
  and a reader who suspects padding skims. Density means each retained page carries argument the
  relief needs; saturation means every available page is full.
- Theory of the case, held honestly: a single organising theme gives the document its spine, but
  never bend, select or soften found facts to preserve the theme. If the facts as found do not fit
  the theory, attack the fact-finding directly or change the theory.

## Adverse Authority And Candour

- Adverse authority must be disclosed: bringing contrary authority to the tribunal's attention
  is an ethical duty, not a tactical choice. The persuasive question is only how it is met.
- Distinguish on the facts first: argue the facts of this case sit outside the adverse
  precedent's reasoning. Attack the correctness of an adverse decision only as a last resort,
  and never attack an authority's worth or the opponent's motives - attack applicability and
  reasoning.
- Confront the obvious weakness up front and in your own words. Volunteering and answering the
  weak point projects command of the case; side-stepping it persuades the reader there is no
  answer. Concede a genuinely non-essential point cleanly - candour is a weapon, and a
  located concession buys credibility for every contested point that follows.
- Anticipate, never attribute. An expected answer is framed as expected ("the claimant may
  answer that ..."), sourced to the record where he has actually said it, and never put into
  the opponent's mouth as a position the record does not contain (see the integrity rules in
  `source-boundary-and-citations.md`).
- Do not take an isolated sentence of the decision below out of context: identify the impugned
  passage precisely (verbatim quote or tight summary, always with the record pinpoint) and meet
  it as it was meant. A reader forced to ask "where do we find that?" has stopped being
  persuaded.
- Explain, do not label: bald characterisations of the other side's argument ("hopeless",
  "nonsense", "misconceived" without more) do none of the tribunal's work. State why the
  argument fails - the premise it needs, and the source that removes that premise.

## Reply Submissions

- A reply answers; it does not re-argue. Take the opponent's distinct points in a sensible
  order, state acceptance or rejection of each, and give the short reason with its pinpoint.
  Do not restate the case-in-chief - cross-refer to it by paragraph.
- Answer the argument actually put, at its strongest reading, not a weaker paraphrase. Meeting
  a straw man concedes the real point silently.
- Silence on a point in reply reads as concession: sweep the opponent's document once, list
  every distinct contention, and account for each - answered, adopted, or expressly not
  pressed and why.
- Hold the whole argument in writing: a point saved for the hearing that has no trace in the
  written submissions carries "an unattractive air of afterthought" and may not be reached at
  all on the papers.
- Anticipate; never attribute. A primary submission may answer points of principle the opponent
  is expected to press, but only framed expressly as anticipation ("X advances, and Y
  anticipates, the following points"), stated neutrally at their strongest, and answered from
  what the record already carries. Never attribute to the opponent an argument no document of
  theirs yet makes. Hold predicted-only answers - points with no present record footprint - for
  the authorised reply round: filing them early previews the reply case and reads as arguing
  with yourself.
- Fold answers into the deciding node; do not build a standalone rebuttal Part. Where the
  opponent has actually served a point, answer it inside the ground or node that decides it - a
  cite to his filed paragraph plus a one-line answer - not in a separate "answers to the points
  against us" section. A standalone anticipation/rebuttal Part duplicates the grounds (inviting a
  state-once breach), and, where a reply round is already timetabled, reads as seeking a leave to
  reply you already have. Reserve a separate section only for genuinely new matter that has no
  home in a ground.

## Sentence Economy

- Average around 20 words. Treat any sentence over about 24 words, or carrying three or more
  list items, as a split-or-tabulate prompt - not a hard rule where precision or a pinpoint
  genuinely needs the length (the canonical statement is in `australian-english-house-style.md`).
- Three or more parallel quantifications, versions or events, each carrying its own source and
  outcome, are a table, not a prose chain (columns on the pattern figure / where advanced /
  basis / fate). Byte-preserve every figure, quote and anchor in the conversion, and leave the
  table unnumbered if nothing cites it, so it never disturbs an existing table series.
- Vary sentence length deliberately; uniform length reads as machine rhythm. Land the decisive
  point in the shortest sentence, after a longer reasoned one.
- Active voice with strong verbs naming the actor; cut nominalisations and agentless passives.
- Plain English: cut `herein`, `said` (as adjective), `pursuant to`, `inter alia`, `the same` (as
  pronoun); replace wordy connectives (`in order to` -> `to`; `due to the fact that` -> `because`;
  `in the event that` -> `if`; `with regard to` -> `about`).
- Cite sparingly: one pinpoint for an uncontested point; reserve full treatment for the central
  contested ground; do not string several cases for a settled proposition.

## A Deliberate Fresh-Reader Pass

- Before finalising, read the document cold as the busy judicial reader. Can you recover relief,
  grounds and reasons from the headings and the first sentence of each paragraph alone? Where
  not, that section fails front-loading - repair it before polishing.

## Boundary (Carry From Concision)

- None of these rules strips an honest legal negative or source boundary - non-service,
  non-admission, under-appeal, a reserved issue. Keep one genuine-boundary qualifier per
  proposition. Positive form, em-dash count 0, contentions not findings, anchors never cut to
  shorten.
